Abstract: This paper examines the risk management techniques employed by international firms, with a particular emphasis on currency, interest rate, and commodity risks. The study emphasizes how crucial derivatives and corporate hedging techniques are for reducing risk. The results highlight the significance of risk management in achieving financial stability and assisting with other financial decision-making. Introduction: Multinational companies as the name implies are the corporations that hold their operations globally. Therefore they are most vulnerable to political, economic, legal, and financial risks. The primary focus of financial risk management in multinational corporations is to identify the risks and take effective measures and strategies beforehand. The main financial risks that multinational companies face are currency risk (exchange rate risk), interest rate risk, and commodity risks. Multinational companies must perform their operations in diverse global markets so monitoring the changing trends is essential. Similarly dealing in foreign currencies also leads to certain complexities. The management must be ready for any risk beforehand. Therefore multinational companies adopt a mixture of financial instruments and operational strategies, from forward contracts to natural hedging, to effectively manage currency risks, interest rate risks, and commodity risks, which helps them maintain their position in the marketplace with resilience. Literature Review: Due to their international activities, multinational corporations (MNCs) confront a variety of financial risks, making financial risk management essential. These hazards, which include shifts in commodity prices, interest rate volatility, and currency fluctuations, can have a big effect on a company's bottom line and ability to compete. Forward contracts, options, and swaps are examples of traditional techniques for financial risk management that offer predictability and protect against unfavorable currency changes. Interest rate swaps and futures contracts are used to control interest rate risk because they stabilize interest costs and lessen exposure to rate volatility. Commodity risk is linked to price fluctuation in production inputs and raw materials, which can have an impact on the profitability and cost structure of a multinational corporation. By fusing risk management with strategic decision-making, effective risk management should allow MNCs to seize growth opportunities and improve their competitive positioning.

   Findings:

   Currency Risk Management
   Currency risk or exchange rate risk means the volatility of exchange rates which as a result affect
   the financial performances of multinational corporations. This makes the corporation dealing in
   foreign currencies more vulnerable to losses. The main purpose of currency risk management is
   to reduce or remove the possibility that a future payment or receipt of foreign currency will be
   made at a different rate by fixing the exchange rate today.
   The currency risk can be managed through the following financial instruments and strategies:
   Forward Contract:
   The forward contract is a binding agreement between any two parties to fix the exchange rate. It
   is also called an over-the-counter agreement because it is flexible which means it can be
   arranged for any currency, amount, and time duration. It is also non-standardized which means it

cannot involve any third party. Under a forward contract, the company buys or sells foreign
currency on the due date as per the forward rate by fixing an exchange rate with the bank
immediately.
Currency Options:
A currency option is a nonbinding agreement with either a bank or dealer in the options market
which gives the option holder the right to buy or the right to sell the foreign currency at a future
date at a price fixed today. It protects against adverse movements in exchange rates as well as
provides an opportunity to take advantage of favorable movements in exchange rates.
The exercise price is the exchange rate fixed between the option holder and the option writer. In
case of foreign currency receipt, the option holder will enter into a call option (option to
purchase) agreement because it gives the option to buy the required foreign currency. The option
contract will be exercised only if the exercise price is higher than the spot rate. In case of foreign
currency payment, the option holder will put into put option (option to sell) agreement because it
gives the option to sell the required foreign currency. The option contract will be exercised if the
exercise price is lower than the spot rate.
Currency Futures:
Currency future is a standardized binding agreement in which no one is buying or selling
anything, only the difference is settled to hedge the currency risk. Currency futures have an
expiry date and it is available for leading currencies. The future contract is always to be sold if
purchased and to be purchased if sold. This is called closing the position. The company has to
close the position in any case either loss or profit since it is a binding agreement.
Future contracts are traded on foreign exchanges which act as marketplaces for buyers and
sellers. Some future markets are the New York Mercantile Exchange (NYMEX), the Chicago
Mercantile Exchange (CME), the Chicago Board of Trade (CBoT), the Cboe Options
Exchange (Cboe), and the Minneapolis Grain Exchange. Traders deposit an initial margin as
a security deposit which is a fraction of the total contract value. If somehow the market moves
against them there will be a margin call which summons the traders to add more funds. Since
futures contracts are marked to market every day, profits and losses are determined using the
closing prices after each trading day.
Interest Rate Risk
When a company deposits or borrows money at a floating rate of interest, the interest rate will
change concerning market rate of interest. This change can affect the corporations positively or
negatively. In Pakistan, the benchmark rate of interest is the KIBOR Karachi interbank offer rate.
Banks use the KIBOR as a base rate and charge interest on loans from their clients at an interest
rate higher than KIBOR. The extra above KIBOR is called credit spread.
Now if the company has borrowed at a variable rate of interest then it will have to bear the
interest costs if the interest rate goes up. However, in the case of a fixed rate of interest, the

corporations still face risks. For example, in the issuance of bonds, the companies have to pay
the same rate of interest even if KIBOR goes down. But if its competitors have issued the bonds
at variable rates of interest, or competitors who decided to issue fixed rate bonds after the rate
has fallen, the competitors will gain an advantage.
Interest rate risk can be managed through:
Forward Rate Agreements (FRA):
A forward rate agreement is a binding agreement to fix the interest rate of future borrowings or
future investments. If a company has taken a loan today or deposited an amount today, then the
rate will be agreed today and in that case, there is no issue. But if the company has to take a loan
after two months and there is a risk that the interest rate might change during that period, then
the company arranged the interest rate today.
Short-Term Interest Rate Futures (STIR):
Interest rate future is a binding agreement which means the company has to close position in any
case either profit or loss. It has a fixed contract size and fixed expiry date. The company will buy
the future today at the current future price. The company will sell future on the due date i.e. at
the start of the investment date at the prevailing future price. If the KIBOR rate moves adversely
i.e. in case the market interest rate is decreased, then the company will cover the loss of the open
market through gain from the future market or vice versa.
Commodity Risk Management
Commodity risk is the possibility that changes in the price of commodities will hurt a company's
financial performance or position. Producers of commodities, such as those in the energy (oil,
gas, and electricity), agricultural (wheat, cotton, sugar), and mining (gold, coal, etc.) sectors, are
most vulnerable to price declines, which implies they will make less money from the
commodities they produce.

Forward and Forward Contracts:
Forward contracts are agreements to buy or sell a commodity on a future date at a predetermined
price, with physical delivery expected and payment at maturity. They lock in a fixed price and
protect against price fluctuations. Futures contracts, similar to forward contracts, are similar but
do not require physical delivery. They are traded on commodity exchanges and settled by
offsetting the contract before maturity. They often require margin calls to meet price movements,
impacting cash flow.
Commodity Options and Commodity Swaps:
Commodity options and commodity swaps are financial transactions used to manage commodity
price risk. Commodity options allow an entity to purchase or sell a commodity with the right but

not the obligation to undertake the transaction at a future date. They insure against adverse price
movements and require a premium.
Discussions:
The findings show that by using derivatives strategically, one may greatly reduce the risk
associated with currency, interest rates, and commodities. However, timely execution and proper
risk assessment are prerequisites for these devices to be effective. Multinational corporations
(MNCs) that include these technologies in a thorough risk management framework typically see
more consistent financial results. Counterparty risks and the challenge of keeping track of so
many financial instruments are the drawbacks of employing financial instruments for risk. Future
research should focus on how these instruments may be used to reduce risk over long run and
how technology can be incorporated into intricate financial management.
Conclusion:
Variations in currency exchange rates, interest rates, and commodity prices pose significant
financial risks to multinational firms. To reduce these risks, it is crucial to use financial products
like swaps, options, and futures strategically in addition to employing efficient risk management
techniques. MNCs may improve their competitive advantage and financial stability by
effectively implementing these techniques.
