## Introduction

Social protection programs represent a society's commitment to its most vulnerable members. In Pakistan, where nearly 22% of the population lives below the national poverty line and millions more hover just above it, the design and effectiveness of these programs is not an academic question — it is a matter of survival.

This paper evaluates the impact of Pakistan's major social protection initiatives on poverty reduction, drawing on available evidence and identifying gaps that future research should address.

## Pakistan's Social Protection Landscape

The country's social protection architecture has evolved significantly over the past fifteen years. The landmark Benazir Income Support Programme (BISP), launched in 2008, was Pakistan's first large-scale unconditional cash transfer program. It has since been expanded and rebranded under the Ehsaas umbrella, which encompasses over 130 policies and programs targeting different dimensions of poverty.

Key components include:

- **Unconditional cash transfers (Kafaalat)**: Regular payments to women in the poorest households, identified through a poverty scorecard (the National Socio-Economic Registry).
- **Conditional education transfers (Waseela-e-Taleem)**: Stipends conditional on school enrollment and attendance for children aged 4-22.
- **Emergency cash assistance**: One-time payments during crises, including the COVID-19 emergency cash program that reached 16.9 million families.
- **Interest-free loans (Waseela-e-Rozgar)**: Microfinance for poverty graduation.

## Evidence of Impact

The evidence on BISP's poverty impact is mixed but cautiously positive. Rigorous evaluations, including those by the Oxford Policy Management group, have found that:

- Cash transfers reduce the depth of poverty (how far below the poverty line recipients fall) more consistently than they reduce the headcount (the number of people below the line).
- Female recipients show improvements in decision-making autonomy and children's school enrollment.
- The program's targeting accuracy has improved with successive iterations of the poverty scorecard, though exclusion errors remain significant.

However, the transfer amount — approximately PKR 9,000 per quarter as of 2023 — has not kept pace with inflation, eroding its real value and limiting its poverty-reduction impact.

## Structural Limitations

Social protection in Pakistan faces several structural challenges:

- **Coverage gaps**: Despite BISP's scale, millions of eligible households remain unregistered or excluded due to data limitations.
- **Fiscal sustainability**: The program's budget, while growing, remains modest relative to the scale of need — approximately 0.8% of GDP.
- **Graduation pathways**: Cash transfers alleviate immediate deprivation but do not, on their own, create pathways out of poverty. Without complementary investments in education, health, and livelihoods, recipients may remain transfer-dependent indefinitely.
- **Administrative fragmentation**: The proliferation of programs under Ehsaas has created coordination challenges and, in some cases, duplication.

## Comparative Perspective

Pakistan's experience can be usefully compared with other developing countries. Brazil's Bolsa Família, Mexico's Prospera (formerly Oportunidades), and India's MGNREGA offer models of how social protection can be linked to human capital development and productive employment.

The key lesson from these comparisons is that cash transfers work best as part of a broader system — one that combines income support with access to quality education, healthcare, and economic opportunity.

## Directions for Future Research

Several important questions remain underexplored in the Pakistani context:

- What is the long-term impact of BISP on intergenerational poverty transmission?
- How effective are graduation models (combining cash transfers with asset transfers and skills training) in the Pakistani context?
- What role can digital financial inclusion play in improving program efficiency and reach?

## Conclusion

Pakistan's social protection programs have achieved meaningful, if incomplete, results. They have reached millions of families who would otherwise have no formal safety net, and they have demonstrated that large-scale cash transfer programs are administratively feasible in low-income settings.

But protecting the vulnerable is not the same as ending poverty. The next frontier is building programs that not only cushion deprivation but create the conditions for sustainable exit from it.
