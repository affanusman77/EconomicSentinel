## Introduction

Food inflation is not an abstraction. It is the difference between a family eating three meals or two, between a child staying in school or being pulled out to work. In Pakistan, where food constitutes nearly 35% of the average household's consumption basket — and over 50% for the poorest quintile — understanding food price dynamics is essential to understanding poverty itself.

This paper draws on the 2022-23 Economic Survey to trace the structural and cyclical drivers of food inflation, and to assess the adequacy of the policy response.

## The Scale of the Problem

The 2022-23 Economic Survey reports that food inflation averaged 36.5% during the fiscal year — a figure that masks significant variation across commodities, geographies, and income groups.

Staples bore the brunt: wheat prices rose by over 45%, cooking oil by 38%, and pulses by nearly 50%. For households already spending the majority of their income on food, these increases represent not just inflation but a direct assault on nutritional security.

## Structural Drivers

Pakistan's food inflation is not purely a monetary phenomenon. It reflects deep structural weaknesses in the agricultural supply chain:

- **Water scarcity and climate vulnerability**: The 2022 floods destroyed nearly 9 million acres of cropland, devastating wheat, rice, and cotton harvests. Climate change is making such events more frequent, not less.
- **Post-harvest losses**: An estimated 30-40% of perishable produce is lost between farm and market due to inadequate cold chain infrastructure, poor road connectivity, and fragmented supply chains.
- **Market concentration**: Middlemen and cartels in key commodity markets extract significant rents, creating a wedge between farmgate and retail prices that neither producers nor consumers benefit from.
- **Import dependence**: For critical items like edible oil and pulses, Pakistan relies heavily on imports, making domestic prices hostage to global commodity cycles and exchange rate movements.

## The Policy Response

The government's response to food inflation has been a mix of administrative measures and fiscal interventions. Price controls, subsidized utility stores, and the Benazir Income Support Programme have been the primary instruments.

Each has limitations. Price controls, when enforced, tend to create shortages and black markets. Utility stores serve only a fraction of the population. Cash transfers, while better targeted, lose real value quickly when the prices they are meant to offset are rising month on month.

## Implications for Young Economists

The food inflation crisis is a reminder that economics is, at its core, about people. The numbers in the Economic Survey are not just data points — they are the lived experience of millions of families navigating an increasingly hostile price environment.

For student researchers, this is fertile ground: there is no shortage of unanswered questions about supply chain reform, targeted social protection, climate-resilient agriculture, and the political economy of food pricing.

## Conclusion

Addressing food inflation in Pakistan requires moving beyond short-term fixes toward structural reforms that improve agricultural productivity, reduce post-harvest losses, and build resilience against climate shocks. The 2022-23 Economic Survey provides the data; what remains is the political will to act on it.
