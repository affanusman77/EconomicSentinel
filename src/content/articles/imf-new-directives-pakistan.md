## Introduction

The International Monetary Fund's engagement with Pakistan has entered a critical new phase. As the country navigates mounting fiscal pressures and a complex inflationary environment, the IMF's directives for the 2024-25 federal budget carry significant weight — not just for policymakers, but for every household managing its finances under tightening conditions.

This paper examines the key directives, their intended economic outcomes, and the trade-offs they impose on a nation balancing stabilization against growth.

## The IMF Framework

The Fund's conditionalities for Pakistan's latest program center on three pillars: fiscal consolidation, exchange rate flexibility, and energy sector reform. Each carries distinct implications for the federal budget and, by extension, for inflation.

### Fiscal Consolidation

The directive to achieve a primary surplus of 0.4% of GDP requires significant revenue mobilization. The 2024-25 budget reflects this through expanded tax net coverage, increased petroleum levy rates, and the withdrawal of several exemptions that had shielded key sectors.

The revenue target of PKR 12.97 trillion represents a 40% year-on-year increase — an ambitious figure that assumes both compliance improvements and economic recovery.

### Exchange Rate Policy

The IMF's preference for a market-determined exchange rate has been a persistent source of tension. While currency flexibility can improve export competitiveness and attract foreign investment, it also feeds directly into imported inflation — a particular concern for an economy dependent on energy and food imports.

## Inflation Dynamics

Pakistan's inflation trajectory tells a story of compounding pressures. Supply-side constraints, currency depreciation, and administered price adjustments have kept headline inflation elevated, even as global commodity prices moderated.

The budget's impact on inflation operates through multiple channels:

- **Direct taxation effects**: Higher indirect taxes on goods and services translate into immediate price increases for consumers.
- **Energy price adjustments**: The phased elimination of subsidies — a core IMF requirement — feeds through to transportation, manufacturing, and agricultural costs.
- **Demand management**: Fiscal tightening, while deflationary in theory, can be offset by the inflationary impact of tax-driven price increases.

## Policy Implications

The fundamental tension in Pakistan's current policy framework is the simultaneous pursuit of stabilization and growth. The IMF's directives prioritize the former, but the social and political sustainability of austerity depends on the latter.

For young economists and students of public policy, this moment offers a case study in constrained optimization: how does a government maximize welfare when its policy toolkit is limited by external conditionalities, domestic political economy, and the arithmetic of debt sustainability?

## Conclusion

The 2024-25 budget, shaped by IMF directives, represents both a necessary correction and a significant gamble. Its success will depend not just on revenue collection and expenditure control, but on the government's ability to communicate the logic of short-term pain for long-term stability — and to protect the most vulnerable from the costs of adjustment.
